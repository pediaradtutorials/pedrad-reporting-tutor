Pediatric Emergency Care Applied Research Network (PECARN) prediction rule of 7
[Ref 27] Holmes et al. 2013
  • No evidence of abdominal wall trauma or evidence of seat belt sign
  • Glasgow Coma Scale score greater than 13
  • No abdominal tenderness
  • No evidence of thoracic wall trauma
  • No complaints of abdominal pain
  • No decreased breath sounds
  • No vomiting
 This prediction rule of seven history and clinical examination parameters (without laboratory or ultrasound findings) identifies patients who are at very low risk for intra-abdominal injury.
Pediatric Surgery Research Collaborative
[Ref 29] Streck et al. 2017
  • AST > 200 U/L
  • Abnormal abdominal examination
  • Abnormal chest radiograph
  • Report of abdominal pain
  • Abnormal pancreatic enzymes
 The rule had a negative predictive value of 99.4% for intra-abdominal injury and 100.0% for intra-abdominal injury requiring intervention in patients with none of the prediction rule variables present.
Blunt pediatric abdominal trauma guideline
[Ref 30] Gaffey et al. 2021

Step 1: Abnormal FAST → CT indicated.

Step 2 (if FAST normal): If all of the following are met — AST < 200, ALT < 100, Hgb > 8.5, and urine analysis 0–5 RBCs/HPF — observe for 12–24 h. Otherwise, CT is indicated.

Step 3 (during observation): If serial abdominal exams are normal with normal WBC, Hgb drop < 1.5, and temperature < 101°F → discharge without CT. Otherwise, CT is indicated.

 The application of an evidence-based clinical guideline reduces the frequency of CT use while precisely detecting serious injuries.
🟡 Role of EFAST in children

EFAST has poor sensitivity in paediatric abdominal trauma. Most valuable in haemodynamically unstable children to identify bloody collections mandating urgent surgery. Never use a negative FAST to exclude injury in a child with clinical suspicion.

🔴 Radiation considerations (ALARA)

The EPI-CT cohort study confirmed a dose-dependent link between paediatric CT and brain cancer risk. Standard: single-phase venous CT. Arterial phase only for suspected vascular injury. Split-bolus technique when dual-phase is needed. Delayed phase only for urinary system — not routine.

Most commonly injured organ in paediatric abdominal trauma. Posterior right lobe most vulnerable. Grade VI avulsion eliminated in 2018 (nonsurvivable — V is highest).

Specific CT findings
Periportal low attenuation — dilated periportal lymphatics from fluid resuscitation. Not a direct injury sign.
Sentinel clot — high-density haematoma (>60 HU) adjacent to liver indicates nearby parenchymal injury.
Biliary injury — rare (0.9–3.7%). Suspect if laceration extends to porta hepatis + contracted gallbladder. MRI/MRCP for biloma detection (delayed up to 60 days).
Bare area injury — causes retroperitoneal haematoma (not peritoneal). Easy to miss.

Non-operative management succeeds in the vast majority of stable children. Surgical rate <6% vs ~20% in adults. Angioembolisation rarely used in paediatric splenic trauma.

Specific CT findings
Splenic cleft vs laceration — Clefts: smooth, linear, may contain fat. Lacerations: irregular outline, surrounded by haematoma.
Splenic shattering — complete fragmentation = grade V. Haemoperitoneum if capsule torn. Blood tracks along splenorenal ligament if hilum injured.
Multiple low-grade injuries — two or more grade I/II injuries in the same organ upgrade to grade III (2018 rule).

Third most commonly injured organ. The 2018 update added segmental vascular injury and renal infarction. Delayed phase essential if injury ≥ grade II.

Special considerations
Delayed phase — if renal laceration ≥ grade II, macroscopic haematuria, pelvic fracture, or perinephric fluid.
Main renal artery injury (grade V) — prompt intervention required. CT shows absent parenchymal enhancement with patent proximal artery stump.
Gonadal vein pitfall — may mimic ureter. Follow anatomical course; confirm with delayed phase.
Bilateral injury upgrade — both kidneys injured at grade I–II → upgrade overall grade by one (max III) per AAST-OIS 2018.

Occurs in ~3–12% of blunt paediatric abdominal trauma. Often from handlebar/seatbelt injury compressing pancreas against spine. Early CT may be normal — serum lipase and MRI/MRCP are key adjuncts.

CT imaging pitfalls
Early CT may be normal — oedema and peripancreatic fluid may not appear for 6–12 hours. Repeat CT or MRI/MRCP if clinical suspicion persists.
Transection vs contusion — look for linear hypoattenuating band across pancreatic neck/body. Peripancreatic fat stranding is an early sensitive sign.
Main pancreatic duct injury — determines grade (≥III). CT unreliable for duct assessment; MRCP or ERCP needed. Duct disruption → pseudocyst/fistula risk.
Handlebar / seatbelt mechanism — high suspicion with epigastric wall bruising. Check lipase at 3–6 h post-injury.
Associated injuries — pancreatic body injuries commonly associated with duodenal injuries (D2–D3 junction).

Occurs in 3–10% of blunt pelvic trauma. CT cystogram required — passive IV contrast filling has poor sensitivity. Two parallel classification systems are used: the AAST Urinary Bladder Injury Scale (surgical/endoscopic basis) and the Radiologic Classification System by Sandler et al. (imaging basis). Each grade card shows both systems side by side.

AAST grades (I–V) — based on surgical/endoscopic findings; size of laceration and anatomical extent determine grade.
Radiologic grades (1–5) — based on CT cystogram findings; preferred for imaging-based classification (Sandler et al.).
CT cystogram technique
Indications — macroscopic haematuria, pelvic ring fracture, perivesical fluid, or renal laceration ≥ grade II.
Technique — retrograde diluted contrast via urethral catheter; adequate bladder distension required. CT with full bladder + post-drainage views.
Extraperitoneal rupture (Radiologic 4a/4b) — 4a: contrast confined to intrapelvic extraperitoneal space. 4b: contrast tracks into anterior abdominal wall, scrotum, penis, or perineum. Associated with pelvic ring fractures. Management: catheter drainage ± surgical repair.
Intraperitoneal rupture (Radiologic 2) — contrast outlines bowel loops and peritoneal recesses. Mechanism: distended bladder + lap belt. Management: surgical repair.
Bladder neck / trigone injury (AAST Grade V) — laceration extending into bladder neck or trigone. Always requires surgical repair. Assess for associated urethral injury.

Select CT findings for each organ to determine the AAST-OIS 2018 grade. The final grade is the highest-ranking criterion. Multiple grade I–II injuries in the same organ are upgraded by one grade (max III).

The ASU+ algorithm provides a systematic stepwise approach to reading paediatric abdominal trauma CT.

A

Air & Active haemorrhage

Lung window: pneumoperitoneum, pneumothorax (free air anterior to liver). Soft tissue window: active bleeding — hyperattenuating blush isodense to arteries, persists on delayed. Sentinel clot (>60 HU).

S

Solid organ injury

Assess liver, spleen, pancreas. Liver window for hepatic parenchyma. Grade using AAST-OIS 2018. Distinguish pseudoaneurysm (washout on delayed) from active bleeding (persistent) from AVF (early venous opacification).

U

Urological injury

Kidneys, ureters, bladder, adrenals, retroperitoneum, aorta, IVC. Delayed phase if renal laceration ≥ grade II, haematuria, or perinephric fluid. CT cystogram if bladder injury suspected.

+

Plus — bowel, peritoneum, bones, hypoperfusion complex

Bowel wall thickening, mesenteric stranding, free fluid. Bone window: ribs, vertebrae, pelvis. Hypoperfusion complex: small aorta/IVC, diffuse bowel/mesentery/adrenal enhancement, periportal hypoattenuation.

Window settings
Lung window
Pneumoperitoneum & PTX

Free air anterior to liver. Check pleural recesses.

Liver window
Hepatic parenchyma

Contusions, lacerations, periportal hypoattenuation.

Soft tissue window
Active bleeding & solid organs

Haemorrhage, lacerations, haematomas, peritoneal fluid.

Bone window
Skeletal injuries

Ribs, vertebrae, pelvis, femur. Rib fractures may mimic organ contusion.

Standardised terminology improves accuracy of diagnosis, grading, and interdisciplinary communication.

Laceration
Linear or branching hypoattenuating cleft through solid organ parenchyma. Depth and length determine grade.
Subcapsular haematoma
Crescentic hypoattenuating collection beneath organ capsule, causing mass effect. Sized by % surface area.
Intraparenchymal haematoma
Rounded hypoattenuating collection within parenchyma. Size measured in diameter. More discrete than contusion.
Contusion
Ill-defined parenchymal hypoattenuation without discrete laceration or haematoma. May become isoattenuating on delayed.
Active haemorrhage
Hyperattenuating blush (isodense to arteries) that persists or increases on delayed images. Does not follow blood pool.
Pseudoaneurysm
Contained vascular injury — well-defined contrast blush that washes out on delayed images (unlike active bleeding).
Arteriovenous fistula
Abnormal artery–vein communication — premature venous opacification simultaneously with arterial phase.
Sentinel clot
High-density haematoma (>60 HU) indicating injury to an adjacent solid organ. Location localises bleeding source.
Organ fracture
Laceration extending from one capsular surface to the opposite, dividing the organ. ≥ grade III for spleen and kidney.
Devascularisation
Non-enhancement of a segment or entire organ. Wedge-shaped = segmental infarct. Total = grade V injury.
Peritoneal fluid
Free fluid in hepatorenal space, paracolic gutters, or pelvis. >30–40 HU = haemoperitoneum. Check all three locations.
Hypoperfusion complex
Signs of hypovolaemic shock: collapsed IVC/aorta, intense bowel/adrenal enhancement, periportal hypoattenuation.

Key differences in management and physiology between paediatric and adult solid organ trauma.

🧒 Children

  • Management determined solely by haemodynamic stability
  • Stable → non-operative regardless of CT grade
  • AAST grade guides hospital stay & activity restriction
  • Surgical rate for spleen: <6%
  • Angioembolisation rarely used for splenic trauma
  • Single-phase (venous) CT standard
  • More radiosensitive — ALARA essential

🧑 Adults

  • CT grade directly influences operative decision
  • Grade III–V → consider angioembolisation
  • Vascular injury → angioembolisation recommended
  • Surgical rate for spleen: ~20%
  • Angioembolisation standard for high-grade splenic injuries
  • Arterial phase often added
  • Grade VI hepatic avulsion was historically described
Why children are more vulnerable
Anatomy: Smaller abdominal cavity, thinner fat, relatively larger organs, weaker abdominal wall → less protection from blunt impact.
Radiation sensitivity: More rapidly dividing cells, smaller body cross-section (higher organ dose), longer lifespan to manifest radiation effects.
Multiple low-grade injuries (2018 rule): Two or more grade I/II injuries in the same organ upgrade to grade III.
CT protocol summary
Standard: Single-phase portal venous, lower thorax to pubic symphysis.
Split-bolus: Combined arterial + venous in one acquisition (2/3 + 1/3 bolus).
Arterial phase: High-velocity/penetrating trauma or suspected arterial injury for endovascular planning only.
Delayed phase: Suspected urinary tract injury or vascular blush on venous phase. Not routine.
